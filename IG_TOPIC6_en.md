## 6.1 Earth and the Solar System

### 6.1.1 Earth, Moon and Sun

#### Earth’s Rotation and Day/Night

- **Rotation**: Earth spins on its axis once every **24 hours**, causing day and night. The axis is tilted by about **23.5°** relative to its orbital plane (the ecliptic).
- **Cause of day/night**: Earth is an opaque sphere; only half is illuminated by the Sun. As Earth rotates, any point on the surface moves into and out of the illuminated half.
- **Apparent motion of the Sun**: Because Earth rotates west to east, the Sun appears to rise in the east, move across the sky, and set in the west.

#### Seasons (Supplement)

- **Orbit and axial tilt**: Earth orbits the Sun once every **365.25 days**. Seasons are **not** caused by changes in Earth‑Sun distance (the orbit is nearly circular). Instead, they result from **Earth’s revolution around the Sun** together with the **axial tilt (23.5°)**.
- **Solar altitude and insolation**: As Earth moves along its orbit, the latitude receiving direct sunlight shifts between the Tropic of Cancer (≈23.5°N) and the Tropic of Capricorn (≈23.5°S). When the Northern Hemisphere tilts toward the Sun, sunlight is more direct (higher solar altitude), and each square metre receives more energy → summer. The Southern Hemisphere then experiences winter.
- **Seasonal markers**:
    - **Equinoxes (spring/autumn)**: Sun directly over the equator; day and night equal.
    - **Summer solstice (Northern Hemisphere)**: Sun over Tropic of Cancer; longest day.
    - **Winter solstice (Northern Hemisphere)**: Sun over Tropic of Capricorn; shortest day.
- **Opposite seasons**: When it is summer in the Northern Hemisphere, it is winter in the Southern Hemisphere.

#### Moon’s Motion and Phases (Supplement)

- **Orbit**: The Moon orbits Earth once every **27.3 days** (sidereal month).
- **Phases cycle**: The cycle from new moon to new moon is about **29.5 days** (synodic month). The difference arises because Earth moves along its orbit during one lunar revolution, so the Moon needs extra time to realign with the Sun.
- **Cause**: Phases are due to the changing relative positions of the Sun, Earth and Moon, which determine how much of the Moon’s illuminated half we see from Earth.

### 6.1.2 Structure and Formation of the Solar System

#### Components of the Solar System

- **Central star**: The Sun – contains over 99% of the system’s mass.
- **Eight planets (in order from the Sun)**: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. Mnemonic: **My Very Excellent Mother Just Served Us Noodles.**
- **Categories**:
    - **Inner terrestrial planets**: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars – small, dense, rocky/metallic.
    - **Outer gas giants**: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune – huge, low density, mainly hydrogen and helium.
    - **Moons**: natural satellites orbiting planets.
    - **Dwarf planets**: like Pluto – orbit the Sun but have not cleared their orbital neighbourhood.
    - **Asteroids and asteroid belt**: many small rocky bodies; most orbit in the **asteroid belt** between Mars and Jupiter.
    - **Comets**: “dirty snowballs” of ice, dust and rock. They travel in **highly elliptical** orbits. When near the Sun, ice sublimates, forming a coma and tail.

#### Formation of the Solar System (Supplement)

- The Solar System formed about **4.6 billion years ago** according to the **accretion model**:
    1. **Nebula**: A cloud of gas and dust began to contract under its own gravity.
    2. **Spinning and heating**: Conservation of angular momentum made the cloud spin faster and flatten into a **protoplanetary disc**. The centre became very hot.
    3. **Sun formation**: When the core reached sufficient temperature and pressure, **nuclear fusion** ignited – the Sun was born.
    4. **Planet formation**: In the hot inner disc, only high‑melting‑point materials (metals, rock) could condense → small, dense **rocky planets**. In the cold outer disc, abundant ices and gases could also accumulate → huge, low‑density **gas giants**.

### 6.1.3 Measurements in Space

#### Astronomical Distance Unit

- **Light‑year (ly)**: the distance light travels in a vacuum in **one year**. It is a unit of distance, not time.
- **Conversion**: 1 ly = 9.5 × 10^15 m

#### Scale of the Universe

- **Milky Way galaxy**: the galaxy containing our Sun – a disc with about 100 billion stars. Its diameter is roughly **100 000 light‑years**.
- **Observable Universe**: contains hundreds of billions of such galaxies.

#### Gravitational Field Strength

- Surface gravity depends on a planet’s **mass** and **radius**: larger mass → stronger; smaller radius → stronger.
- Gravitational field strength decreases with **distance** from the centre.

#### Light Travel Time (Supplement)

- Time = distance / speed of light: `t = d / c`, where `c = 3.0 × 10^8 m/s`.

### 6.1.4 Gravity and Orbits

#### Orbital Motion

- **Central force**: The gravitational attraction between a planet and the Sun (or moon and planet) provides the centripetal force needed for orbital motion.
    - `F ∝ (m1 * m2) / r^2` (qualitative only; inverse‑square law).
- **Speed for nearly circular orbits**: average orbital speed = circumference / period.
    - `v = 2 * π * r / T`
    - `v`: orbital speed (m/s)
    - `r`: orbital radius (m)
    - `T`: orbital period (s)
    - **Warning**: `r` is the distance from the **centre** of the central body. If the problem gives altitude above the surface, add the radius of the central body.

#### Elliptical Orbits (Supplement)

- Planets, dwarf planets and comets orbit in **ellipses**, not perfect circles. For planets the orbit is nearly circular; comets have **highly elliptical** orbits.
- **Kepler’s second law (qualitative)**: A body moves **fastest** at perihelion (closest to the Sun) and **slowest** at aphelion (farthest from the Sun).
- **Energy explanation**: Gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy interchange. At perihelion (smallest distance), potential energy is lowest → kinetic energy highest → greatest speed; at aphelion the reverse is true.

### 6.1.5 Analysing Planetary Data (Supplement)

You may be given a table of planetary data (mass, diameter, density, surface temperature, surface gravitational field strength, number of moons, etc.) and asked to compare properties. Typical observations: gas giants are much more massive and larger but much less dense than terrestrial planets; they also tend to have many more moons.

## 6.2 Stars and the Universe

### 6.2.1 The Sun

- The Sun is an **average‑sized main‑sequence star**. Its composition is roughly **73% hydrogen** and **25% helium**.
- Energy comes from **nuclear fusion** (hydrogen fusing into helium) in its core.
- The Sun emits radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum, but most energy is in the **infrared, visible and ultraviolet** bands.

### 6.2.2 Stellar Evolution (Supplement)

#### Early stages (same for all stars)

- **Nebula → Protostar → Main‑sequence star**
    1. **Nebula**: A large cloud of gas and dust collapses under gravity.
    2. **Protostar**: As it contracts, the core becomes denser and hotter. It becomes a hot, dense gas ball – a protostar.
    3. **Main‑sequence star**: When core temperature reaches about 15 million °C, hydrogen fusion starts. The outward **thermal pressure** balances inward **gravity**. The star is now stable and called a main‑sequence star.

#### Late stages of low‑ and medium‑mass stars (less than 8 solar masses)

- **Red giant**: Hydrogen in the core runs out; the core contracts and heats up, causing the outer layers to expand → red giant.
- **Planetary nebula + white dwarf**: The red giant ejects its outer layers → planetary nebula. The hot, dense core left behind is a **white dwarf** (mainly carbon and oxygen).
- **Black dwarf**: After billions of years, the white dwarf cools and fades. No black dwarf yet exists because the universe is too young.

#### Late stages of high‑mass stars (more than 8 solar masses)

- **Red supergiant**: Successive fusion stages (carbon, oxygen, neon, silicon) produce heavier elements until iron is formed.
- **Supernova**: Iron fusion consumes energy rather than releasing it. The core collapses violently, triggering a **supernova explosion** that blasts the star’s outer layers into space.
- **Neutron star or black hole**: The remnant core:
    - If the remnant mass is about 3 solar masses → **neutron star** (made of tightly packed neutrons).
    - If even more massive → gravity completely collapses it into a **black hole** (nothing, not even light, can escape).
- **Recycling**: Supernovae produce and scatter heavy elements (especially those heavier than iron), which become part of new stars and planetary systems.

### 6.2.3 Cosmology (Supplement)

#### Redshift

- **Observation**: Spectral lines from a distant object are shifted toward longer wavelengths (the **red** end) if the object is moving away from us; toward shorter wavelengths (blue shift) if moving toward us.
- **Cause**: Doppler effect. For light, a receding source → wavelength stretched → redshift.
- **Galaxy redshifts**: Almost all distant galaxies show redshift – they are moving away from us. The farther a galaxy is, the greater its redshift.

#### Expanding Universe and Hubble’s Law

- **Hubble’s law**: A galaxy’s recession speed `v` is proportional to its distance `d` from Earth.
    - `v = H_0 * d`
    - `v`: recession speed (km/s)
    - `H_0`: **Hubble constant** (currently accepted value is **2.2 × 10^–18 per second**)
    - `d`: distance to the galaxy (km) – memorise `H_0` as given.
- **Evidence for expansion**: Redshift and Hubble’s law imply that galaxies are moving apart → the Universe is expanding uniformly.

#### Age of the Universe

- **Approximation**: If expansion had been constant (ignoring gravity), age ≈ `1 / H_0`.
    - `1 / H_0` yields about **13.8 billion years**. This matches more precise measurements and supports the Big Bang theory.
- **Starting point**: Running time backwards leads to a singularity where all matter and energy were concentrated – the **Big Bang**.

#### Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) – Supplement

- **Definition**: Uniform microwave radiation filling all space.
- **Connection to Big Bang**: CMBR is the “afterglow” of the hot, dense early Universe. As the Universe expanded, the original high‑energy radiation stretched into the microwave region.
- **Significance**: CMBR is one of the strongest pieces of evidence for the Big Bang, providing a “baby picture” of the Universe about 380 000 years after its birth.

## Common Exam Traps & Hints

- **Day/night vs seasons**: Day/night caused by **rotation**; seasons by **revolution + axial tilt**.
- **Galactic recession vs stellar motion**: Recession due to cosmic expansion (Hubble flow) is on the scale of galaxies, not stars within a galaxy.
- **Orbital radius**: Always use **distance from the centre** of the central body. If altitude (height above surface) is given, **add the radius** of the central body.
- **Post‑main‑sequence evolution**: Must distinguish low‑mass (like the Sun) from high‑mass stars.
    - **Low‑mass (≤ 8 M☉)**: red giant → planetary nebula + white dwarf → black dwarf.
    - **High‑mass (> 8 M☉)**: red supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole.
- **Nebula**: The same term describes both the birthplace of stars (star‑forming nebula) and the ejected envelope of a dying star (planetary nebula). They are physically different.
