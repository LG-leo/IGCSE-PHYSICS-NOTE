# TOPIC 1: MOTION, FORCES AND ENERGY

## 1.1 Physical Quantities and Measurement Techniques

### 1.1.1 Length and Volume
- Use a ruler to measure length (mm/cm/m).
- Use a measuring cylinder to measure volume of liquids (cm³/ml).

### 1.1.2 Time
- Use clocks or digital timers to measure time intervals.
- To measure short intervals accurately, measure multiple cycles and find the average.
- Example: For a pendulum, measure time for 20 oscillations, then divide by 20 to find the period (no numerical example given).

### 1.1.3 Scalars and Vectors
- **Scalars**: only magnitude (distance, speed, time, mass, energy, temperature).
- **Vectors**: magnitude + direction (force, weight, velocity, acceleration, momentum, electric field strength, gravitational field strength).

#### Vector Addition
- For two vectors at right angles:
  - Magnitude: Pythagoras theorem \( \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \)
  - Direction: trigonometry \( \tan \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} \)
- Methods: calculation or graphical scale drawing (parallelogram law).

## 1.2 Motion

### Speed and Velocity
- Speed = distance / time, \( v = s/t \)
- Velocity = speed in a given direction (vector)
- Average speed = total distance / total time

### Distance-Time Graphs
- Horizontal line → at rest
- Straight diagonal → constant speed
- Curve → accelerating or decelerating
- Gradient = speed

### Speed-Time Graphs
- Horizontal line → constant speed
- Straight diagonal → constant acceleration
- Curve → changing acceleration
- Gradient = acceleration
- Area under graph = distance travelled

### Free Fall
- Acceleration of free fall \( g \approx 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2 \) near Earth’s surface
- Constant for all objects (ignoring air resistance)

### Acceleration
- \( a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \) (change in velocity / time taken)
- Vector quantity
- Deceleration = negative acceleration

### Terminal Velocity
- Without air resistance: constant acceleration
- With air resistance:
  1. Initially: weight > air resistance → acceleration
  2. Speed increases → air resistance increases
  3. Eventually: air resistance = weight → resultant force = 0
  4. Terminal velocity reached (constant speed)

## 1.3 Mass and Weight

- **Mass**: quantity of matter, measured in kg, scalar, constant everywhere.
- **Weight**: gravitational force on an object, measured in N, vector, varies with location.
- Gravitational field strength \( g = W/m \), numerically equal to acceleration of free fall (9.8 N/kg on Earth).
- Use a balance to compare masses or weights.

---

## 1.4 Density

- Definition: \( \rho = m / v \) (density = mass / volume)
- Units: g/cm³ or kg/m³

### Measuring Density
- Liquid: measuring cylinder for volume, balance for mass.
- Regular solid: measure dimensions → calculate volume, balance for mass.
- Irregular solid (sinks): displacement method (record initial water volume, submerge object, record final volume, volume = final - initial).

### Floating and Sinking
- Object floats if its density < liquid density.
- Object sinks if its density > liquid density.
- For two immiscible liquids: the less dense liquid floats on top.

## 1.5 Forces

### 1.5.1 Effects of Forces

#### Force Effects
- Change shape (deformation)
- Change size (stretch/compress)
- Change motion (speed or direction)

#### Hooke’s Law – Load‑Extension Graphs
- Initial straight line: extension ∝ load (obeys Hooke’s law)
- Spring constant \( k = F / x \) (force per unit extension)

#### Resultant Force
- Forces in same direction: add
- Forces in opposite direction: subtract

#### Newton’s First Law
- An object at rest stays at rest.
- An object moving at constant speed stays at that speed.
- Unless acted on by a resultant force.

#### Friction and Drag
- Friction: between two solid surfaces.
- Drag: when object moves through liquid or gas (air resistance is a type of drag).
- Both oppose motion and produce heating.

#### Limit of Proportionality
- The point on a load‑extension graph beyond which extension is no longer proportional to load (not the same as elastic limit).

#### Newton’s Second Law
- \( F = m a \) (F is resultant force)
- Force and acceleration are in the same direction.

#### Circular Motion
- A force perpendicular to motion keeps object moving in a circle – centripetal force.
- Factors: mass, speed, radius.
  - Mass ↑ or speed ↑ → force ↑
  - Radius ↓ → force ↑

### 1.5.2 Turning Effect of Forces

#### Moment of a Force
- \( \text{Moment} = F \times d \) where \( d \) is the perpendicular distance from pivot to line of action of force.

#### Principle of Moments
- For an object in equilibrium: sum of clockwise moments = sum of anticlockwise moments.

#### Conditions for Equilibrium
1. No resultant force.
2. No resultant moment.

### 1.5.3 Centre of Gravity

#### Definition
- The point where the whole weight of an object appears to act.

#### Finding Centre of Gravity (Experiment)
1. Hang lamina freely from a point.
2. Hang plumb line from same point.
3. Draw line along plumb line.
4. Repeat from different point.
5. Intersection of lines = centre of gravity.

#### Stability
- Lower centre of gravity → more stable.
- Wider base → more stable.

## 1.6 Momentum

### Definition
- \( p = m \times v \) (momentum = mass × velocity)
- Vector quantity (direction same as velocity)
- Unit: kg·m/s

### Impulse
- Impulse = \( F \times \Delta t \) (force × time)
- Impulse = change in momentum \( \Delta (mv) \)
- Force = rate of change of momentum: \( F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} \)

### Conservation of Momentum
- In a closed system (no external forces): total momentum before = total momentum after.
- For collisions in one dimension: \( m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 \).

## 1.7 Energy, Work and Power

### 1.7.1 Energy

#### Energy Stores
- Kinetic
- Gravitational potential
- Chemical
- Elastic / strain
- Nuclear
- Electrostatic
- Internal / thermal

#### Energy Transfers
- By forces (mechanical work)
- By electric currents (electrical work)
- By heating
- By waves (electromagnetic, sound)

#### Conservation of Energy
- Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed.
- Total energy in any process is constant.

#### Kinetic Energy
- \( E_k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \)

#### Gravitational Potential Energy
- \( \Delta E_p = m g \Delta h \)

### 1.7.2 Work

- Work done = energy transferred.
- Mechanical work: \( W = F \times d \)
- Unit: joule (J)

### 1.7.3 Energy Resources

| Source                | Method                                                                 | Renewable? |
|-----------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------|
| Fossil fuels          | Burn → boiler → steam → turbine → generator                            | No         |
| Biofuels              | Burn similarly                                                         | Yes        |
| Hydroelectric         | Falling water turns turbine                                            | Yes        |
| Waves / Tides         | Water movement turns turbine                                           | Yes        |
| Geothermal            | Heat from underground → steam → turbine                                | Yes        |
| Nuclear fuel          | Nuclear fission heats water → steam → turbine                          | No         |
| Solar cells           | Light → electricity directly                                           | Yes        |
| Solar panels          | Infrared heats water                                                   | Yes        |
| Wind                  | Wind turns turbine                                                     | Yes        |

#### Considerations
- Renewability, availability, reliability, scale, environmental impact.

#### Efficiency
- Efficiency = useful energy output / total energy input (qualitative understanding).

#### Main Energy Source
- Sun is main source for all except geothermal, nuclear, tidal.

#### Nuclear Fusion in Sun
- Hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium, releasing enormous energy.

#### Efficiency Formula
- Efficiency = (useful energy output / total energy input) × 100%
- Efficiency = (useful power output / total power input) × 100%

### 1.7.4 Power

- Power = work done / time
- Power = energy transferred / time
- \( P = W / t \) and \( P = \Delta E / t \)
- Unit: watt (W)

## 1.8 Pressure

- \( P = F / A \) (pressure = force / area)
- Unit: Pa (N/m²)

### Applications
- Small area → large pressure (e.g., knife blade)
- Large area → small pressure (e.g., snowshoes)

### Liquid Pressure
- Increases with depth.
- Increases with density of liquid.
- Change in pressure: \( \Delta p = \rho g \Delta h \)

# TOPIC 2: THERMAL PHYSICS

## 2.1 States of Matter

### 2.1.1 Properties of Solids, Liquids and Gases

| Property          | Solid                 | Liquid                    | Gas                        |
|-------------------|-----------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| Shape             | Fixed                 | Takes shape of container  | No fixed shape             |
| Volume            | Fixed                 | Fixed                     | Takes volume of container  |
| Particle arrangement | Regular pattern       | Random but close          | Far apart                  |
| Particle separation | Very close            | Close                     | Very far apart             |
| Particle motion   | Vibrate about fixed positions | Move around each other    | Move randomly at high speed |

### 2.1.2 Changes of State

- Melting (solid → liquid)
- Boiling (liquid → gas)
- Condensation (gas → liquid)
- Solidification / Freezing (liquid → solid)
- Sublimation (solid → gas, not required in Core but mentioned in some contexts)

During changes of state, temperature remains constant while energy is used to break or form bonds between particles.

---

## 2.2 Particle Model

### 2.2.1 Particle Structure Summary

- **Solid**: regular pattern, particles vibrate in place, strong forces between particles.
- **Liquid**: random arrangement, particles can move past each other, weaker forces than solids.
- **Gas**: particles far apart, fast random motion, negligible forces between particles.

### 2.2.2 Temperature and Particle Motion

- Higher temperature → particles have more kinetic energy.
- Absolute zero = –273 °C (0 K) – lowest possible temperature.
- At absolute zero, particles have minimum kinetic energy (still move slightly – zero point energy).

### 2.2.3 Gas Pressure

- Caused by particles colliding with surfaces.
- Each collision exerts a tiny force.
- Many collisions per second → measurable pressure.
- Pressure = force / area.

### 2.2.4 Brownian Motion

- Random motion of microscopic particles (e.g., pollen grains, smoke particles) suspended in a fluid.
- Caused by constant collisions with faster‑moving molecules of the fluid.
- Provides evidence for the kinetic particle model (particles are moving randomly).

### 2.2.5 Forces Between Particles

- Solids: strong forces, particles in fixed positions.
- Liquids: weaker forces, particles can move.
- Gases: negligible forces, particles independent.

### 2.2.6 Pressure Explained in Detail

- Gas particles collide with walls of container.
- Each collision causes a change in momentum.
- Force = rate of change of momentum.
- Pressure = total force / area.

### 2.2.7 Distinguish: Atoms/Molecules vs Microscopic Particles

- Atoms/molecules: too small to see, make up all matter.
- Microscopic particles: visible under microscope (e.g., smoke particles), made of many atoms/molecules.

---

## 2.3 Gases and the Absolute Scale of Temperature

### 2.3.1 Effect on Gas Pressure (Fixed Mass)

| Condition                         | Effect on pressure | Particle explanation                                      |
|-----------------------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|
| Temperature ↑ at constant volume  | Pressure ↑         | Particles move faster → more frequent and harder collisions |
| Volume ↑ at constant temperature  | Pressure ↓         | Particles hit walls less often (longer distance between collisions) |

### 2.3.2 Temperature Conversion

- \( T (\text{K}) = \theta (\text{°C}) + 273 \)
- Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero.
- Examples: 0 °C = 273 K, 100 °C = 373 K, –273 °C = 0 K.

### 2.3.3 Boyle's Law (Supplement)

- For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature: \( p \times V = \text{constant} \).
- Graphical representations:
  - \( p \) against \( V \): inverse curve.
  - \( p \) against \( 1/V \): straight line through origin.

## 2.4 Thermal Properties and Temperature

### 2.4.1 Thermal Expansion of Solids, Liquids and Gases

#### What is Thermal Expansion?
- When heated at constant pressure, most materials expand.
- Particles gain kinetic energy and move further apart.

#### Everyday Examples

| Example                                  | Explanation                                                   |
|------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Gaps in railway tracks                   | Allow rails to expand on hot days without buckling.          |
| Expansion joints in bridges              | Prevent damage from thermal expansion.                        |
| Bimetallic strip in thermostats          | Two different metals expand at different rates, causing bending. |

#### Explaining Relative Expansion (Particle Model)

| State  | Expansion | Reason                                                                 |
|--------|-----------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Solids | Least     | Particles vibrate more but stay in position; strong forces limit expansion. |
| Liquids | Medium    | Particles can move apart more easily.                                  |
| Gases  | Greatest  | Particles move much faster; negligible forces allow large separation increase. |

Order of expansion: Gases ≫ Liquids ≫ Solids.

### 2.4.2 Specific Heat Capacity

#### Internal Energy
- When temperature rises, internal energy increases.
- Internal energy = total kinetic energy + potential energy of particles.

#### Temperature and Particle Energy
- Temperature increase = increase in average kinetic energy of all particles.
- Not all particles have same energy – distribution changes.

#### Specific Heat Capacity Definition
- Specific heat capacity \( c \) = energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C.
- Formula: \( c = \frac{\Delta E}{m \times \Delta \theta} \)
- Units: J/(kg·°C) or J/(g·°C).

#### Measuring Specific Heat Capacity (Experimental setup – no numbers)
- For a solid: measure mass, insulate, use electric heater to supply known energy, measure temperature rise, calculate \( c \).
- For a liquid: similar but use an insulated container (calorimeter).
- Key precautions: insulation to reduce heat loss, stirring to ensure even temperature, use digital thermometer for accuracy.

### 2.4.3 Melting, Boiling and Evaporation

#### Melting and Boiling
- During melting or boiling, temperature remains constant.
- Energy input is used to:
  - Break bonds between particles.
  - Overcome forces of attraction.
- Latent heat = energy absorbed/released during change of state.

#### Melting and Boiling Points of Water
- Melting point: 0 °C (at standard atmospheric pressure).
- Boiling point: 100 °C (at standard atmospheric pressure).

#### Condensation and Solidification
- Reverse processes of boiling and melting.
- Particles lose energy, bonds form between particles.

#### Evaporation
- Escape of more‑energetic particles from liquid surface.
- Occurs at any temperature.
- Causes cooling because high‑energy particles leave, so remaining particles have lower average kinetic energy.

#### Boiling vs Evaporation

| Feature             | Boiling                         | Evaporation                     |
|---------------------|---------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| Temperature         | Occurs at fixed boiling point   | Occurs at any temperature       |
| Location            | Throughout the liquid           | Only at the surface             |
| Energy requirement  | Needs continuous energy input   | Occurs naturally                |
| Rate                | Fast                            | Slow                            |
| Bubbles             | Form within liquid              | No bubbles                      |

#### Factors Affecting Evaporation Rate

| Factor                | Effect          | Reason                                                                 |
|-----------------------|-----------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Higher temperature    | Rate ↑          | More particles have enough energy to escape.                          |
| Larger surface area   | Rate ↑          | More particles at surface can escape.                                 |
| Increased air movement| Rate ↑          | Removes vapour above surface, maintaining concentration gradient.     |
| Lower humidity        | Rate ↑          | Steeper concentration gradient for vapour to diffuse away.            |

#### Cooling by Evaporation – Detailed Explanation
1. Particles with highest kinetic energy escape from liquid surface.
2. Remaining particles have lower average kinetic energy.
3. Temperature of liquid decreases.
4. Liquid absorbs heat from surroundings (e.g., skin) to replace lost energy.
5. This is why sweat cools you down.

## 2.5 Transfer of Thermal Energy

### 2.5.1 Conduction

#### Demonstrating Conduction
- **Experiment 1 – Metal Rod with Pins**: attach pins to metal rod with wax, heat one end; pins drop in order as heat conducts along rod – shows conduction through metal.
- **Experiment 2 – Comparing Materials**: test different materials as handles (copper, glass, wood); copper gets hot quickly (good conductor), wood stays cool (poor conductor / insulator).

#### Mechanism of Conduction

##### In All Solids – Lattice Vibrations
- Particles vibrate more when heated.
- Vibrations passed to neighbouring particles.
- Energy transfers through material.

##### In Metals – Free Electrons
- Metals have delocalised (free) electrons.
- Electrons move rapidly through metal.
- Collide with particles and transfer energy.
- This is why metals are the best conductors.

#### Why Conduction is Poor in Gases and Most Liquids
- Particles are far apart.
- Few collisions between particles.
- Energy transfer is slow.

#### Good Conductors vs Insulators
- Many solids conduct better than insulators but worse than good conductors (e.g., stainless steel conducts better than wood, worse than copper).

### 2.5.2 Convection

#### What is Convection?
- Main method of heat transfer in fluids (liquids and gases).
- Involves movement of fluid itself.

#### How Convection Works
1. Fluid is heated.
2. Heated fluid expands → becomes less dense.
3. Less dense fluid rises.
4. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to take its place.
5. Circulation current (convection current) is set up.

#### Demonstrating Convection
- **In water**: put potassium permanganate crystals at bottom of beaker, heat gently; coloured water rises, showing convection current.
- **In air**: use a smoke box with a candle; smoke shows the convection path.

### 2.5.3 Radiation

#### What is Thermal Radiation?
- Infrared radiation.
- All objects emit it.
- Does NOT require a medium – can travel through vacuum.
- Travels at the speed of light.

#### Effect of Surface

| Surface        | Absorption | Emission | Reflection |
|----------------|------------|----------|------------|
| Black, dull    | Good       | Good     | Poor       |
| White, shiny   | Poor       | Poor     | Good       |

#### Everyday Examples
- Solar panels are black to absorb more radiation.
- Kettles are shiny to reduce heat loss by radiation.
- White clothes in summer reflect radiation.

#### Constant Temperature
- For an object at constant temperature: energy received = energy emitted.
- If energy received > energy emitted → temperature rises.
- If energy received < energy emitted → temperature falls.

#### Earth's Temperature Balance
- Incoming solar radiation (short wavelength).
- Outgoing infrared from Earth's surface (long wavelength).
- Balance affected by:
  - Clouds (reflect some radiation).
  - Greenhouse gases (absorb and re‑emit infrared).
  - Surface type (ice reflects more, forests absorb more).

#### Experiments to Compare Emission/Absorption
- **Leslie's Cube**: cube with different surfaces (black dull, white shiny, etc.); fill with hot water; use infrared detector to measure radiation from each side – black dull side emits most.
- **Absorption experiment**: two thermometers with different coatings, place under heat lamp; black‑coated thermometer shows higher temperature.

#### Factors Affecting Emission Rate
- Surface temperature ↑ → rate ↑.
- Surface area ↑ → rate ↑.

## 2.6 Consequences of Thermal Energy Transfer

### 2.6.1 Everyday Applications

#### (a) Heating Kitchen Pans
- Conduction through metal base and walls.
- Convection in liquid inside.
- Radiation from heat source (if gas flame or electric coil).

#### (b) Heating a Room by Convection
- Radiator heats air nearby.
- Warm air rises.
- Cool air sinks to replace it.
- Convection current circulates around room.

### 2.6.2 Complex Applications (Supplement)

#### (a) Fire Burning Wood/Coal
- Radiation: from flames to surroundings.
- Convection: hot air and gases rise up chimney.
- Conduction: through solid fuel, through grate to firebox.

#### (b) Radiator in a Car
- Conduction: from engine to coolant (water/antifreeze).
- Convection: in coolant as it circulates through engine and radiator.
- Radiation: from radiator surface to air.
- Convection: of air around radiator (helped by fan).

# TOPIC 3: WAVES

## 3.1 General Properties of Waves

### 3.1.1 What are Waves?
- Waves transfer energy from one place to another.
- They do NOT transfer matter (particles vibrate but stay in approximately the same position).
- Examples: water waves, sound waves, electromagnetic waves, seismic waves.

### 3.1.2 Demonstrating Wave Motion
- Ropes and springs: create waves by flicking one end – observe pulses and continuous waves.
- Water waves (ripple tank): observe wave patterns on water surface, reflection, refraction, diffraction.

### 3.1.3 Wave Features

| Term          | Symbol | Definition                                                | Chinese     |
|---------------|--------|-----------------------------------------------------------|-------------|
| Wavefront     | -      | Line joining points on wave in same phase (e.g., all crests) | 波阵面      |
| Wavelength    | λ (lambda) | Distance between successive crests or troughs            | 波长        |
| Frequency     | f      | Number of complete waves passing a point per second       | 频率        |
| Crest / Peak  | -      | Highest point of a wave                                   | 波峰        |
| Trough        | -      | Lowest point of a wave                                    | 波谷        |
| Amplitude     | A      | Maximum displacement from rest position                   | 振幅        |
| Wave speed    | v      | Speed at which wave energy travels                        | 波速        |

### 3.1.4 Wave Equation
- \( v = f \times \lambda \)
- Wave speed = frequency × wavelength
- Units: v in m/s, f in Hz, λ in m

### 3.1.5 Types of Waves

| Type               | Direction of vibration                     | Examples                                                                 |
|--------------------|--------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Transverse waves   | Perpendicular (at right angles) to wave direction | Electromagnetic radiation, water waves, seismic S‑waves                   |
| Longitudinal waves | Parallel to wave direction                 | Sound waves, seismic P‑waves, ultrasound                                 |

### 3.1.6 Wave Phenomena
All waves can undergo:
1. **Reflection** – bouncing off a surface.
2. **Refraction** – changing direction due to change in speed.
3. **Diffraction** – spreading out when passing through a gap or around an edge.

### 3.1.7 Ripple Tank Experiments

| Phenomenon        | How to demonstrate                                                                 |
|-------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Reflection        | Place barrier at angle; observe waves bouncing off.                               |
| Refraction        | Change water depth (shallower → slower speed); observe wavelength change and direction change. |
| Diffraction (gap) | Place barrier with narrow gap; observe waves spreading out after gap.             |
| Diffraction (edge)| Place barrier with single edge; observe waves bending around edge.                |

### 3.1.8 Factors Affecting Diffraction (Supplement)

#### Through a gap
- Diffraction is most noticeable when gap width ≈ wavelength.
- If gap is much larger than wavelength → little diffraction.
- If gap is smaller than wavelength → strong diffraction (waves spread out in circles).

#### At an edge
- Longer wavelengths diffract more (bend more around edges).
- Shorter wavelengths travel more in straight lines.

#### Summary table

| Gap size relative to λ | Diffraction effect                                      |
|------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| Gap ≫ λ                | Very little diffraction                                 |
| Gap ≈ λ                | Maximum diffraction                                     |
| Gap ≤ λ                | Waves spread out almost circularly                      |

## 3.2 Light

### 3.2.1 Reflection of Light

#### Key Terms

| Term               | Definition                                                      | Chinese |
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|---------|
| Normal             | Line perpendicular to reflecting surface at point of incidence | 法线    |
| Angle of incidence (i) | Angle between incident ray and normal                         | 入射角  |
| Angle of reflection (r) | Angle between reflected ray and normal                        | 反射角  |

#### Law of Reflection
- Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (\( i = r \))
- Incident ray, reflected ray and normal all lie in the same plane.

#### Image Formation by Plane Mirror
Characteristics of the image:
- Same size as object.
- Same distance behind mirror as object is in front.
- Virtual – cannot be projected on a screen.
- Laterally inverted (left‑right reversed).
- Upright.

#### Ray Diagrams for Plane Mirrors (Supplement)
1. Draw object in front of mirror.
2. Draw at least two rays from object to mirror.
3. Apply law of reflection \( i = r \).
4. Extend reflected rays behind mirror (dashed lines).
5. Where dashed lines meet = position of virtual image.

#### Calculations
- Image distance = object distance.
- Can use scale drawings to find image position.

---

### 3.2.2 Refraction of Light

#### Experiment to Show Refraction
1. Place rectangular glass block on paper.
2. Shine ray of light at an angle into block.
3. Mark incident ray, emergent ray.
4. Remove block, draw lines, measure angles.

#### What is Refraction?
- Change in direction of light when it passes from one medium to another.
- Caused by change in speed of light.
- Light bends towards the normal when slowing down (entering denser medium).
- Light bends away from the normal when speeding up (entering less dense medium).

#### Critical Angle
- The angle of incidence in the denser medium that produces an angle of refraction of 90° in the less dense medium.
- Only occurs when light travels from denser to less dense medium.

#### Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
- Occurs when:
  1. Light travels from denser to less dense medium.
  2. Angle of incidence > critical angle.
- All light is reflected back into the denser medium.
- No light passes through the boundary.

#### Everyday Examples of TIR
- Prisms in binoculars.
- Optical fibres.
- Mirage effects (not required in Core).

#### Refractive Index (Supplement)

##### Definition
- \( n = \frac{\text{speed of light in vacuum}}{\text{speed of light in medium}} \)
- \( n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} \) (for light entering denser medium from air/vacuum)
  - \( i \) = angle in air/vacuum, \( r \) = angle in medium.
- \( n = \frac{1}{\sin c} \) where \( c \) = critical angle.

#### Optical Fibres

##### Structure
- Core (high refractive index) surrounded by cladding (low refractive index).
- Light travels down core by total internal reflection.

##### Applications
- Telecommunications (telephone/internet signals).
- Endoscopes (see inside body).
- Advantages: high data rate, no interference, thin and flexible.

### 3.2.3 Thin Lenses

#### Types of Lenses

| Lens Type          | Shape           | Effect on parallel light                                      |
|--------------------|-----------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Converging lens    | Thicker in middle | Brings rays together at focal point (focal length positive)   |

| Term                 | Definition                                                       | Chinese |
|----------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|---------|

2. Central ray (through optical centre): goes straight without bending.
3. Focal ray (through near focal point): emerges parallel.

- Rays diverge after lens.
- Extrapolate rays backwards to find virtual image.
- Image is: upright, enlarged, virtual.

#### Magnifying Glass
- Single converging lens used with object inside focal point.

### 3.2.4 Dispersion of Light

#### What is Dispersion?
- Splitting of white light into its component colours.
- Done by passing through a glass prism.

#### The Visible Spectrum (in order of decreasing wavelength / increasing frequency)
- Red
- Orange
- Yellow
- Green
- Blue
- Indigo
- Violet

#### Why Dispersion Happens
- Different colours of light travel at slightly different speeds in glass.
- They refract by different amounts.
- Violet slows down most → bends most.
- Red slows down least → bends least.

#### Monochromatic Light (Supplement)
- Light of a single frequency (e.g., laser light).
- Does NOT disperse (all colours bend same amount).

## 3.4 Sound

### 3.4.1 Production of Sound
- Produced by vibrating sources.
- Examples: vocal cords, loudspeaker cone, musical instrument strings.

### 3.4.2 Nature of Sound Waves
- Longitudinal waves.
- Particles vibrate parallel to direction of energy transfer.

### 3.4.3 Audible Frequency Range
- Humans can hear approximately: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz).
- Below 20 Hz = infrasound.
- Above 20 kHz = ultrasound.

### 3.4.4 Medium Required
- Sound needs a medium to travel.
- Cannot travel through vacuum.
- Speed depends on medium:
  - Fastest in solids.
  - Slower in liquids.
  - Slowest in gases.

### 3.4.5 Speed of Sound in Air
- Approximately 330–350 m/s (varies with temperature).
- Much slower than light (hence we see lightning before hearing thunder).

### 3.4.6 Determining Speed of Sound – Echo Method
1. Make a loud sound facing a distant wall.
2. Start timer, stop when echo heard.
3. Distance to wall = \( d \), total distance travelled = \( 2d \).
4. Speed = \( 2d / \text{time} \).

### 3.4.7 Loudness and Pitch

|----------------|-----------------|--------------------------------------|
| Loudness (响度) | Amplitude       | Larger amplitude → louder sound      |

### 3.4.8 Echo
- Reflection of sound waves.
- Used in: sonar, ultrasound scanning, echo location.

### 3.4.9 Ultrasound
- Sound with frequency above 20 kHz.
- Cannot be heard by humans.

### 3.4.10 Compression and Rarefaction (Supplement)

|--------------|----------------------------------------------------------|---------|

| Rarefaction  | Region where particles are spread apart (low pressure)    | 疏部    |

- In longitudinal waves, compressions and rarefactions travel through medium.
- Distance between successive compressions = wavelength.

### 3.4.11 Speed of Sound in Different Media

| Medium | Speed    | Reason                                                       |
|--------|----------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Solids | Fastest  | Particles close together, strong forces, rapid energy transfer |
| Liquids| Medium   | Particles close but forces weaker                            |
| Gases  | Slowest  | Particles far apart, fewer collisions                        |

### 3.4.12 Uses of Ultrasound

| Application                  | How it works                                                                 |
|-----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Non‑destructive testing     | Send ultrasound into material; reflections from cracks/flaws detected.      |
| Medical scanning (sonography) | Ultrasound reflected from different tissues; computer builds image.         |
| Sonar                        | Send pulse to sea bed; measure time for echo; calculate depth.              |

### 3.4.13 Sonar Calculation
- distance = speed × time / 2
- Divide by 2 because sound travels there and back.

# TOPIC 4: ELECTRICITY AND MAGNETISM

## 4.1 Simple Phenomena of Magnetism

### 4.1.1 Magnetic Poles
- Every magnet has a north pole (N) and a south pole (S).
- Like poles repel (N‑N or S‑S).
- Unlike poles attract (N‑S).

### 4.1.2 Magnetic Materials
Materials that are attracted to magnets:
- Iron
- Steel
- Cobalt
- Nickel
These are called ferrous materials.

### 4.1.3 Induced Magnetism
- When a magnetic material is placed near/in contact with a magnet, it becomes a magnet itself.
- The end nearer the magnet becomes opposite pole (attraction).
- Induced magnetism is temporary.

### 4.1.4 Temporary vs Permanent Magnets

| Property          | Temporary Magnet | Permanent Magnet |
|-------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Material          | Soft iron        | Steel            |
| Magnetised        | Easily           | With difficulty  |
| Demagnetised      | Easily           | With difficulty  |
| Retains magnetism | No               | Yes              |

### 4.1.5 Magnetic vs Non‑Magnetic Materials
- Magnetic materials: can be magnetised (iron, steel, cobalt, nickel).
- Non‑magnetic materials: cannot be magnetised (aluminium, copper, plastic, wood).

### 4.1.6 Magnetic Field
- Region around a magnet where a magnetic pole experiences a force.
- Represented by field lines (magnetic field lines).

### 4.1.7 Magnetic Field Lines Around a Bar Magnet
- Direction is from N pole to S pole (outside the magnet).
- Lines never cross.
- Closer lines = stronger field.
- Arrows show direction of force on a N pole.

### 4.1.8 Plotting Magnetic Fields

| Method          | How it works                                                              |
|-----------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Compass         | Place compass at various points; mark direction of N pole; draw line joining points. |
| Iron filings    | Sprinkle on paper over magnet; tap gently; filings align along field lines. |

### 4.1.9 Uses of Magnets

| Type             | Examples                                                             |
|------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Permanent magnets | Compass needles, fridge magnets, loudspeakers, electric motors.      |
| Electromagnets    | Scrap yard cranes, relays, electric bells, MRI machines.             |

### 4.1.10 Magnetic Forces (Supplement)
- Magnetic forces are due to interactions between magnetic fields.
- When two magnets interact, their fields combine.

### 4.1.11 Field Strength (Supplement)
- Represented by spacing of field lines.
- Closer lines = stronger field.
- Further apart = weaker field.

## 4.2 Electrical Quantities

### 4.2.1 Electric Charge

#### Types of Charge
- Positive and negative.
- Like charges repel.
- Unlike charges attract.

#### Charging by Friction
- Rub two insulators together.
- Electrons transfer from one material to the other.
- Material that loses electrons becomes positively charged.
- Material that gains electrons becomes negatively charged.

#### Example of Charging (no numbers)
- Polythene rod rubbed with cloth:
  - Electrons transfer from cloth to polythene.
  - Polythene becomes negatively charged.
  - Cloth becomes positively charged.

#### Detecting Charge – Gold‑Leaf Electroscope
1. Charge is transferred to the metal cap.
2. Charges spread down to gold leaf.
3. Leaf and stem have same charge → they repel.
4. Leaf rises.
5. More charge → greater deflection.

#### Conductors and Insulators

| Conductors              | Insulators                |
|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| Allow charge to flow    | Do not allow charge to flow |
| Metals (copper, aluminium) | Plastic, rubber, glass, wood |
| Have free electrons     | No free electrons         |

#### Electron Model of Conduction
- In metals, some electrons are free – not bound to any particular atom.
- These electrons can move through the metal.
- This is why metals conduct electricity.

#### Charge Unit (Supplement)
- Charge is measured in coulombs (C).

#### Electric Field (Supplement)
- Region where an electric charge experiences a force.
- Direction = direction of force on a positive test charge.

#### Electric Field Patterns (Supplement)

| Configuration                                      | Field pattern                                                              |
|----------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Around a point charge / charged conducting sphere | Radial lines (outwards for +, inwards for –)                              |
| Between two oppositely charged parallel plates    | Uniform field: parallel, equally spaced lines from + to –                 |

### 4.2.2 Current

#### What is Current? (Core)
- Flow of electric charge.
- In metals, current is flow of free electrons.

#### Ammeters
- Used to measure current.
- Connected in series.
- Available as analogue (needle) or digital (display).

#### Types of Current

| Type                    | Meaning                                      | Chinese     |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------------------|-------------|
| Direct current (d.c.)   | Current flows in one direction only          | 直流电      |
| Alternating current (a.c.) | Current repeatedly reverses direction     | 交流电      |

- Mains electricity is a.c. (50 Hz in most countries).
- Cells and batteries supply d.c.

#### Current Definition and Formula (Supplement)
- Current = charge passing a point per unit time.
- \( I = Q / t \)
  - \( I \) = current (A)
  - \( Q \) = charge (C)
  - \( t \) = time (s)

#### Conventional Current (Supplement)
- Direction: from positive to negative (+ to –).
- This is opposite to electron flow (electrons flow from – to +).
- We use conventional current in circuit diagrams.

### 4.2.3 Electromotive Force (e.m.f.) and Potential Difference (p.d.)

#### Definitions (Core)
- e.m.f. (electromotive force) = energy supplied per unit charge by a source (cell/battery).
- p.d. (potential difference) = energy transferred per unit charge across a component.
- Unit for both: volt (V).

#### Voltmeters
- Used to measure p.d.
- Connected in parallel across a component.

### 4.2.4 Resistance

#### Definition (Core)
- Resistance = opposition to current flow.
- Unit: ohm (Ω).
- \( R = V / I \)

#### Experiment to Determine Resistance (no numbers)
1. Set up circuit with cell, ammeter (in series), resistor, voltmeter (in parallel).
2. Measure current (I) and voltage (V).
3. Calculate \( R = V / I \).
4. Repeat with different voltages (using variable resistor) to check consistency.

#### Factors Affecting Resistance of a Wire
| Factor                            | Effect on resistance | Reason                                             |
|-----------------------------------|----------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Longer wire                       | Greater resistance   | Electrons collide with more atoms.                |
| Thicker wire (larger cross‑sectional area) | Smaller resistance | More paths for electrons to flow.               |

#### Current‑Voltage (I‑V) Graphs (Supplement)

##### (a) Resistor (constant resistance)
- Straight line through origin.
- Current ∝ voltage (Ohmic conductor).
- Gradient = 1 / R.

##### (b) Filament lamp
- Curve: gradient decreases as V increases.
- Resistance increases as temperature increases.
- Hotter filament → atoms vibrate more → more collisions.

##### (c) Diode
- Very low resistance in forward direction (allows current).
- Very high resistance in reverse direction (blocks current).
- Only conducts when p.d. exceeds threshold (≈0.6 V for silicon).

#### Relationships for Metallic Conductor
- Resistance ∝ length: \( R \propto L \)
- Resistance ∝ 1 / cross‑sectional area: \( R \propto 1/A \)
- Combined: \( R = \rho L / A \) (ρ = resistivity – depends on material)

### 4.2.5 Electrical Energy and Electrical Power

#### Energy Transfer in Circuits (Core)
- Source (cell/mains) supplies energy.
- Energy transferred to components.
- Components transfer energy to surroundings (heat, light, motion, sound).

#### Electrical Power
- \( P = I \times V \)
- Power = current × voltage.
- Unit: watt (W).

#### Electrical Energy
- \( E = I \times V \times t \)
- Energy = current × voltage × time.
- Unit: joule (J).

#### Kilowatt‑hour (kWh)
- Energy used by a 1 kW device in 1 hour.
- 1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.
- Used for calculating electricity bills.

### 4.3.2 Series and Parallel Circuits

#### Series Circuit

**Characteristics**
- Current is same at every point.
- Voltage splits between components.
- If one component fails, circuit breaks (all stop).
- Total resistance = sum of individual resistances.

**Formulas**
- \( I_{\text{total}} = I_1 = I_2 = I_3 \)
- \( V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 \)
- \( R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 \)

#### Parallel Circuit

**Characteristics**
- Current splits between branches.
- Voltage is same across each branch.
- Each component operates independently.
- Total resistance is less than smallest individual resistance.

**Formulas**
- \( I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3 \)
- \( V_{\text{total}} = V_1 = V_2 = V_3 \)

#### Combined e.m.f. of Sources in Series
- Same direction: add e.m.f.s.
- Opposite direction: subtract.

#### Advantages of Connecting Lamps in Parallel
1. Each lamp operates independently (one can be on while others off).
2. If one lamp fails, others stay on.
3. Each receives full mains voltage (brighter).

#### Kirchhoff’s Laws (Supplement)

**Junction Law (Current Law)**
- Sum of currents entering a junction = Sum of currents leaving.
- Charge is conserved.

**Loop Law (Voltage Law)**
- Total p.d. across components in series = sum of individual p.d.s.
- p.d. across parallel branches is the same.

#### Parallel Resistance Formula (Supplement)
- For two resistors in parallel: \( \frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} \)
- For multiple resistors: \( \frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \ldots \)

### 4.3.3 Action and Use of Circuit Components

#### Relationship for Constant Current (Core)
- For constant current, p.d. across a conductor increases as its resistance increases.
- \( V = I R \) (if I constant, V ∝ R).

#### Variable Potential Divider (Supplement)

**Using a rheostat**
- Connect as shown.
- Moving slider gives variable output voltage from 0 to \( V_{\text{supply}} \).

**Using two fixed resistors**
- Output voltage depends on ratio of resistances.

#### Potential Divider Equation
- \( \frac{R_1}{R_2} = \frac{V_1}{V_2} \)
  - \( V_1 \) is p.d. across \( R_1 \), \( V_2 \) is p.d. across \( R_2 \).
- Output voltage \( V_{\text{out}} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \times V_{\text{supply}} \).

#### Applications
- Volume control.
- Light dimmer.
- Sensor circuits (with LDR or thermistor).

## 4.5 Electromagnetic Effects

### 4.5.1 Electromagnetic Induction

#### What is Electromagnetic Induction? (Core)
- An e.m.f. (voltage) is induced in a conductor when:
  1. It moves across a magnetic field.
  2. The magnetic field linking with it changes.

#### Demonstration Experiment
1. Connect coil to sensitive galvanometer (centre‑zero).
2. Move magnet into coil → needle deflects one way.
3. Move magnet out of coil → needle deflects opposite way.
4. Faster movement → larger deflection.
5. Stronger magnet → larger deflection.
6. More turns on coil → larger deflection.

#### Factors Affecting Induced e.m.f. Magnitude
1. Speed of movement (faster → larger e.m.f.).
2. Strength of magnetic field (stronger → larger e.m.f.).
3. Number of turns on coil (more turns → larger e.m.f.).

#### Direction of Induced e.m.f. (Supplement)
- The induced e.m.f. opposes the change that caused it.
- This is Lenz’s Law.

#### Fleming’s Right‑Hand Rule (Generator Rule) (Supplement)
- For generators: used to find direction of induced current.
- Thumb = Motion (direction of movement).
- First finger = Field (direction of magnetic field, N to S).
- Second finger = Current (direction of induced current).
- Hold so fingers are mutually perpendicular.

---

### 4.5.2 The a.c. Generator

#### Principle (Core)
- An a.c. generator produces alternating current by rotating a coil in a magnetic field (or rotating a magnet near a fixed coil).

#### Two Designs (Supplement)
1. Rotating coil in magnetic field.
2. Rotating magnet near fixed coil.

#### Components
- Coil.
- Magnetic field.
- Slip rings and brushes.

#### How it works
1. Coil rotated in magnetic field.
2. As coil cuts field lines, e.m.f. induced.
3. After half turn, direction of cutting reverses → induced current reverses.
4. Slip rings allow connection to external circuit while rotating.
5. Output is a.c.

#### e.m.f.‑Time Graph
- Peaks: when coil horizontal (cutting field fastest).
- Zeros: when coil vertical (cutting no field).
- Sine wave shape.

---

### 4.5.3 Magnetic Effect of a Current

#### Magnetic Field Around a Straight Wire (Core)
- Pattern: concentric circles around the wire.
- Closer to wire → stronger field.
- Further from wire → weaker field.

#### Direction – Right‑Hand Grip Rule
- Thumb = direction of current.
- Fingers curl = direction of magnetic field.

#### Magnetic Field of a Solenoid (Core)
- Pattern: similar to bar magnet.
- One end acts as N pole, other as S pole.
- Field inside solenoid is strong and uniform.

#### Experiment to Plot Fields
- Use compasses or iron filings.
- Trace direction of field lines.

#### Uses of Electromagnets

| Device        | How it works                                                                 |
|---------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Relay         | Small current in coil magnetises core; attracts armature; closes switch in high‑current circuit. |
| Loudspeaker   | Varying current in coil creates varying magnetic field; interacts with permanent magnet; moves cone; produces sound. |

#### Factors Affecting Field Strength (Supplement)

| Factor                 | Effect              |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Increasing current     | Stronger field      |
| More turns on coil     | Stronger field      |
| Adding soft iron core  | Much stronger field (iron becomes magnetised) |

#### Reversing Current
- Reverses direction of magnetic field.

---

### 4.5.4 Force on a Current‑Carrying Conductor

#### Motor Effect (Core)
- A current‑carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force.
- This is called the motor effect.

#### Demonstration Experiment
1. Place wire between poles of a magnet.
2. Connect to d.c. supply.
3. Wire jumps (experiences force).
4. Reverse current → force reverses.
5. Reverse magnetic field → force reverses.

#### Fleming’s Left‑Hand Rule (Motor Rule) (Supplement)
- For motors: used to find direction of force.
- Thumb = Motion (direction of force/movement).
- First finger = Field (direction of magnetic field, N to S).
- Second finger = Current (direction of conventional current).
- Hold so fingers are mutually perpendicular.

#### Force on Charged Particles (Supplement)
- Same rule applies to beams of charged particles (electrons, protons).
- But remember: electron flow is opposite to conventional current.

---

### 4.5.5 The d.c. Motor

#### Basic Principle (Core)
- Current‑carrying coil in magnetic field experiences a turning effect (torque).
- Forces on opposite sides are in opposite directions → coil rotates.

#### Increasing Turning Effect
1. More turns on coil.
2. Larger current.
3. Stronger magnetic field.

#### Operation of d.c. Motor (Supplement)

**Components**
- Coil on axle between magnet poles.
- Split‑ring commutator – two half‑rings.
- Brushes – carbon blocks touching commutator.

**How it works**
1. Current flows into coil via brushes and commutator.
2. Forces on coil sides produce rotation.
3. After half turn, commutator reverses current direction.
4. Forces now in same direction as before → coil continues rotating same way.

**Why split‑ring commutator is needed**
- Without it, coil would rotate back and forth (oscillate).
- Commutator ensures current direction reverses every half turn.
- This maintains continuous rotation in one direction.

---

### 4.5.6 The Transformer

#### Construction (Core)
- Two coils: primary and secondary.
- Wound on a soft iron core.
- Coils are insulated from each other and from core.

#### Types

| Type               | Turns ratio       | Effect                               |
|--------------------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Step‑up transformer| \( N_s > N_p \)   | Increases voltage (\( V_s > V_p \))   |
| Step‑down transformer | \( N_s < N_p \) | Decreases voltage (\( V_s < V_p \))   |

#### Transformer Equation
- \( \frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} \)
  - \( V \) = voltage, \( N \) = number of turns.
  - p = primary, s = secondary.

#### Use in Power Transmission (Core)
1. Power station: step‑up transformer increases voltage for transmission.
2. Transmission lines: high voltage (low current) reduces energy loss.
3. Near consumers: step‑down transformers reduce voltage to safe levels.

#### Advantages of High‑Voltage Transmission
- For same power transmitted, higher voltage means lower current.
- Lower current means less energy loss in cables (as heat).
- Power loss \( = I^2 R \).

#### Principle of Operation (Supplement)
1. a.c. in primary creates changing magnetic field in core.
2. Iron core channels magnetic field to secondary coil.
3. Changing magnetic field induces an alternating e.m.f. in secondary.
4. Only works with a.c. (needs changing field).

#### Ideal Transformer (100% Efficiency) (Supplement)
- Power in = Power out.
- \( I_p \times V_p = I_s \times V_s \).
- So \( \frac{I_p}{I_s} = \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} \).

#### Power Loss in Cables (Supplement)
- \( P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 \times R \)
  - \( I \) = current in cable, \( R \) = resistance of cable.
- High voltage → low current → much lower power loss (since \( I^2 \)).

# TOPIC 5: NUCLEAR PHYSICS

## 5.1 The Nuclear Model of the Atom

### 5.1.1 The Atom

#### Structure of the Atom (Core)
- An atom consists of:
  - A positively charged nucleus at the centre.
  - Negatively charged electrons orbiting the nucleus.

#### Relative Sizes (Core)
- Nucleus diameter: ~10⁻¹⁵ m.
- Atom diameter: ~10⁻¹⁰ m.
- Nucleus is about 1/10,000th the size of the atom.
- Most of the atom is empty space.

#### Ion Formation (Core)
- Atom loses electron(s) → positive ion.
- Atom gains electron(s) → negative ion.

---

### 5.1.2 The Nucleus

#### Composition of the Nucleus (Core)
- Contains protons and neutrons.
- Protons and neutrons are collectively called nucleons.

#### Relative Charges and Masses (Core)

| Particle  | Relative Charge | Relative Mass | Chinese |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|---------|
| Proton    | +1              | 1             | 质子    |
| Neutron   | 0               | 1             | 中子    |
| Electron  | –1              | ~0 (1/1840)   | 电子    |

#### Atomic Number and Mass Number (Core)

| Term                  | Symbol | Definition                                | Chinese         |
|-----------------------|--------|-------------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Proton number / Atomic number | Z      | Number of protons in the nucleus          | 质子数/原子序数 |
| Nucleon number / Mass number   | A      | Total number of protons + neutrons        | 核子数/质量数   |

- Number of neutrons = A – Z.

#### Nuclei Notation (Core)
- Written as: \( {}^{A}_{Z}\mathrm{X} \)
- Example: \( {}^{14}_{6}\mathrm{C} \) (Carbon‑14)
  - Proton number = 6
  - Nucleon number = 14
  - Neutrons = 14 – 6 = 8

#### Isotopes (Core)
- Atoms of the same element (same proton number Z).
- With different numbers of neutrons (different nucleon number A).
- Same chemical properties (same electron configuration).
- Different physical properties (mass, stability).
- Example: Carbon‑12 and Carbon‑14.
  - Both have Z = 6.
  - C‑12: A = 12 (6 neutrons).
  - C‑14: A = 14 (8 neutrons).

#### Alpha Particle Scattering Experiment (Supplement)
- Conducted by Rutherford, Geiger, Marsden.
- Fired alpha particles at thin gold foil.

##### Observations and Conclusions

| Observation                                               | Conclusion                                         |
|-----------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Most alpha particles passed straight through              | Atom is mostly empty space.                        |
| Some alpha particles deflected through large angles       | Nucleus is small and dense – contains most mass.   |
| A few alpha particles rebounded back (almost 180°)        | Nucleus is positively charged – repels positive alpha particles. |

#### Nuclear Charge and Mass (Supplement)
- Proton number (Z) determines the positive charge on the nucleus: +Ze.
- Nucleon number (A) determines the relative mass of the nucleus (protons and neutrons have similar mass; electron mass negligible).

#### Nuclear Fission (Supplement)
- Splitting of a heavy nucleus into two smaller nuclei.
- Releases energy.
- Example: Uranium‑235 absorbs a neutron → splits into Barium and Krypton + more neutrons + energy.

#### Nuclear Fusion (Supplement)
- Joining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus.
- Releases even more energy than fission.
- Example: Hydrogen nuclei fuse to form Helium in the Sun.

## 5.2 Radioactivity

### 5.2.1 Detection of Radioactivity

#### Background Radiation (Core)
- Low‑level radiation that is always present in the environment.
- Comes from natural and man‑made sources.

##### Sources of Background Radiation

| Source              | Examples                                                     | Chinese                     |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Radon gas           | From rocks and soil, in air                                  | 氡气                        |
| Rocks and buildings | Granite contains radioactive elements                        | 岩石和建筑物                |
| Food and drink      | Potassium‑40 in bananas, etc.                                | 食物和饮料                  |
| Cosmic rays         | From space                                                   | 宇宙射线                    |
| Medical/industrial  | X‑rays, nuclear power (small contribution)                   | 医疗/工业                   |

#### Detection Equipment (Core)
- Geiger‑Müller tube connected to a counter.
- Measures count rate in:
  - counts per second (counts/s)
  - counts per minute (counts/min)

#### Corrected Count Rate (Supplement)
- To find radiation from a source only:
  - Corrected count rate = Measured count rate – Background count rate.
- Always measure background radiation first (with no source present).

### 5.2.2 The Three Types of Nuclear Emission

#### Nature of Radioactive Emissions (Core)

| Property        | Alpha (α)                     | Beta (β)                     | Gamma (γ)                    |
|-----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Nature          | Helium nucleus (2p + 2n)      | Fast‑moving electron         | Electromagnetic wave         |
| Symbol          | α or \( {}^{4}_{2}\mathrm{He} \) | β or \( {}^{0}_{-1}\mathrm{e} \) | γ                            |
| Charge          | +2                            | –1                           | 0                            |
| Mass            | 4 u                           | ~0 (1/1840 u)                | 0                            |

#### Ionising Effect (Core)

| Emission | Ionising effect | Reason                                                                 |
|----------|-----------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Alpha    | Strongly ionising | Large mass, +2 charge → rips electrons from atoms easily.             |
| Beta     | Moderately ionising | Small mass, –1 charge → less interaction.                            |
| Gamma    | Weakly ionising | No charge, interacts by chance collisions.                            |

#### Penetrating Ability (Core)

| Emission | Stopped by                                   | Range in air        |
|----------|----------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Alpha    | Paper, skin, few cm of air                   | ~5 cm               |
| Beta     | Few mm of aluminium, 1 m of air              | ~1 m                |
| Gamma    | Thick lead or concrete (never fully stopped) | Several metres / infinite |

#### Spontaneous and Random (Core)
- Spontaneous: cannot be controlled or influenced (heating/pressure doesn't affect).
- Random: cannot predict which nucleus will decay next.

#### Deflection in Electric and Magnetic Fields (Supplement)

| Emission | Electric field deflection                                          | Magnetic field deflection                         |
|----------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Alpha    | Deflected slightly towards negative plate (opposite to beta).     | Deflected slightly (opposite to beta).           |
| Beta     | Deflected more towards positive plate.                            | Deflected more.                                   |
| Gamma    | No deflection.                                                    | No deflection.                                    |

#### Why Ionising Effects Differ (Supplement)

| Factor           | Alpha                     | Beta                        | Gamma                        |
|------------------|---------------------------|-----------------------------|------------------------------|
| Kinetic energy   | High, but transfers quickly | Moderate                    | High but rarely interacts    |
| Electric charge  | +2 (strong attraction for electrons) | –1 (weaker attraction) | 0 (no direct interaction)    |

### 5.2.3 Radioactive Decay

#### What is Radioactive Decay? (Core)
- A change in an unstable nucleus.
- Results in emission of α or β particles and/or γ radiation.
- Nucleus changes to that of a different element.

#### Key Properties (Core)
- Spontaneous – cannot be controlled.
- Random – unpredictable.

#### Why Some Isotopes are Radioactive (Supplement)

| Reason                       | Example                     |
|------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Too many neutrons in nucleus | Carbon‑14, Tritium (³H)     |
| Nucleus is too heavy         | Uranium‑238, Plutonium      |

#### Effects of Decay on the Nucleus

##### Alpha Decay
- Nucleus loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons.
- Proton number decreases by 2.
- Nucleon number decreases by 4.
- New element is two places left in periodic table.
- General equation: \( {}^{A}_{Z}\mathrm{X} \rightarrow {}^{A-4}_{Z-2}\mathrm{Y} + \alpha \)
- Example: \( {}^{238}_{92}\mathrm{U} \rightarrow {}^{234}_{90}\mathrm{Th} + \alpha \)

##### Beta Decay
- A neutron changes into a proton and an electron.
- Neutron → Proton + Electron (+ antineutrino – not required).
- Proton number increases by 1.
- Nucleon number unchanged.
- New element is one place right in periodic table.
- General equation: \( {}^{A}_{Z}\mathrm{X} \rightarrow {}^{A}_{Z+1}\mathrm{Y} + \beta \)
- Example: \( {}^{14}_{6}\mathrm{C} \rightarrow {}^{14}_{7}\mathrm{N} + \beta \)

##### Gamma Emission
- Nucleus loses energy only.
- Proton and nucleon numbers unchanged.
- Often occurs after α or β decay to remove excess energy.

#### Summary Table for Decay Equations (Exam Focus)

| Type   | General rule                          | Example                                      |
|--------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| α decay | Parent (A,Z) → Daughter (A‑4, Z‑2) + α | U‑238 (238,92) → Th‑234 (234,90) + α        |
| β decay | Parent (A,Z) → Daughter (A, Z+1) + β   | C‑14 (14,6) → N‑14 (14,7) + β               |

#### Key Points for Exams
- α decay: mass number ↓4, proton number ↓2.
- β decay: mass number unchanged, proton number ↑1.
- β particle symbol: β or β⁻.
- In equations, β can be written as \( {}^{0}_{-1}\mathrm{e} \).

## 5.3 Radioactive Decay Calculations (Supplement)

### 5.3.1 Half‑Life

#### Definition
- Half‑life (\( T_{1/2} \)) is the time taken for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay.
- Also the time taken for the count rate to fall to half its initial value.

#### Random Nature
- Half‑life is a statistical average – cannot predict which nucleus will decay next.

#### Graphical Determination
- Plot count rate against time.
- Read the time for count rate to halve from any starting point.

#### Calculation of Remaining Nuclei
- \( N = N_0 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{n} \)
  - \( N_0 \) = initial number of nuclei.
  - \( n \) = number of half‑lives elapsed.
  - \( N \) = remaining number of nuclei.

#### Calculation of Count Rate
- \( A = A_0 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{n} \)
  - \( A_0 \) = initial activity (count rate).
  - \( A \) = activity after \( n \) half‑lives.

#### Finding Number of Half‑Lives
- \( n = \frac{t}{T_{1/2}} \)
  - \( t \) = total time elapsed.
  - \( T_{1/2} \) = half‑life.

#### Example (conceptual, no numbers)
- A radioactive sample has half‑life 1 hour.
- After 1 hour, half remains.
- After 2 hours, quarter remains.
- After 3 hours, one‑eighth remains.

#### Uses of Half‑Life
- Carbon‑14 dating: half‑life ~5730 years – used to date organic remains.
- Uranium‑238 dating: half‑life ~4.5 billion years – used to date rocks.
- Medical tracers: short half‑life (e.g., technetium‑99m, 6 hours) – minimises patient exposure.

## 5.4 Safety and Uses of Radioactivity

### 5.4.1 Hazards of Ionising Radiation (Core)

#### Biological Effects
- Ionising radiation can damage living cells.
- Can cause:
  - Cell death (acute exposure).
  - Mutation (may lead to cancer).
  - Genetic damage (passed to offspring).

#### Factors Affecting Harm
- Type of radiation (alpha most damaging internally, gamma most penetrating externally).
- Energy of radiation.
- Duration of exposure.
- Distance from source.
- Shielding.

#### Protection Measures

| Measure               | Purpose                                                      |
|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Distance              | Stay far from source → reduces exposure (inverse square law). |
| Shielding             | Use lead or concrete for gamma; plastic/glass for beta; paper for alpha. |
| Time                  | Minimise time near source.                                   |
| Containment           | Seal radioactive materials in containers.                    |
| Personal protective equipment | Gloves, goggles, lab coats.                           |

## 5.5 Nuclear Energy

### 5.5.1 Nuclear Fission (Supplement)

#### Process
- A heavy nucleus (e.g., U‑235) absorbs a slow neutron.
- Becomes unstable and splits into two smaller nuclei (fission fragments).
- Releases 2‑3 neutrons and a large amount of energy.

#### Chain Reaction
- Released neutrons can cause further fissions in nearby nuclei.
- If each fission causes at least one more fission → self‑sustaining chain reaction.

#### Control in Nuclear Reactors
- Control rods (boron/cadmium) absorb excess neutrons to control reaction rate.
- Moderator (graphite/water) slows down neutrons to increase fission probability.
- Coolant (water/CO₂) removes heat to generate steam for turbines.

#### Advantages and Disadvantages

| Advantages                                      | Disadvantages                                         |
|-------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| No CO₂ emissions during operation.              | Radioactive waste remains dangerous for thousands of years. |
| High energy density (small fuel mass).          | Risk of nuclear accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima).     |
| Reliable base‑load power (not weather‑dependent). | High construction and decommissioning costs.        |
| Fuel is relatively abundant.                    | Nuclear proliferation risks.                          |

### 5.5.2 Nuclear Fusion (Supplement)

#### Process
- Two light nuclei (e.g., hydrogen isotopes) combine to form a heavier nucleus.
- Releases even more energy per mass than fission.
- Occurs naturally in stars (Sun).

#### Example
- Deuterium + Tritium → Helium + neutron + energy.
- \( {}^{2}_{1}\mathrm{H} + {}^{3}_{1}\mathrm{H} \rightarrow {}^{4}_{2}\mathrm{He} + {}^{1}_{0}\mathrm{n} + \text{energy} \)

#### Challenges for Controlled Fusion on Earth
- Requires extremely high temperatures (~100 million K) to overcome electrostatic repulsion.
- Requires high density and confinement time.
- Currently not commercially viable (research ongoing, e.g., ITER).

#### Advantages of Fusion (If Achieved)
- Abundant fuel (deuterium from seawater, lithium for tritium).
- Very low radioactive waste (reactor components become activated, but waste decays quickly).
- No risk of runaway reaction (plasma cools instantly if containment fails).
- No CO₂ emissions.

# TOPIC 6: SPACE PHYSICS

## 6.1 The Earth and the Solar System

### 6.1.1 The Earth

#### Earth's Motion (Core)
- The Earth is a planet that rotates on its axis.
- The axis is tilted at about 23.5°.
- Rotation period: approximately 24 hours (one day).

#### Effects of Earth's Rotation (Core)
- Apparent daily motion of the Sun: Sun appears to move across the sky from east to west.
- Day and night cycle: the side facing the Sun experiences day; the side away from the Sun experiences night.

#### Earth's Orbit (Core)
- Earth orbits the Sun once in approximately 365 days (one year).
- This orbital period defines a year.

#### Seasons (Core)
- Caused by the tilt of Earth's axis, not by changing distance from the Sun.
- When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun → summer in the north, winter in the south.
- When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun → winter in the north, summer in the south.

#### The Moon (Core)
- The Moon orbits the Earth once in approximately one month (about 27.3 days for sidereal month, but lunar phases cycle about 29.5 days).
- This causes the phases of the Moon: new moon, crescent, half, gibbous, full moon, etc.

#### Average Orbital Speed (Supplement)
- For an object in circular orbit: \( v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} \)
  - \( r \) = average radius of orbit.
  - \( T \) = orbital period.

---

### 6.1.2 The Solar System

#### The Sun (Core)
- The Sun is a star at the centre of the Solar System.
- Contains most of the mass of the Solar System → keeps planets in orbit.

#### Planets (Core)
- There are eight planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.
- Planets are divided into terrestrial (rocky) planets and gas giants.

#### Other Bodies (Core)
- Dwarf planets (e.g., Pluto).
- Asteroids: rocky bodies, mostly in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.
- Comets: icy bodies with elliptical orbits; develop tails when near the Sun.

#### Light Travel Time (Core)
- Time = distance / speed of light.
- Speed of light \( c = 3.0 \times 10^8 \) m/s.

#### Elliptical Orbits (Supplement)
- Planets, minor planets, and comets have elliptical orbits, not perfectly circular.
- The Sun is not at the centre of the ellipse (except when orbit is nearly circular).

#### Orbital Data Analysis (Supplement)
- Candidates should be able to interpret planetary data:
  - Orbital distance (from Sun).
  - Orbital period (duration of one orbit).
  - Density.
  - Surface temperature.
  - Gravitational field strength at surface.

#### Orbital Speed and Distance (Supplement)
- The strength of the Sun's gravitational field decreases with distance.
- Therefore, orbital speeds of planets decrease as distance from Sun increases.
- Example: Mercury orbits fastest, Neptune slowest.

#### Speed in Elliptical Orbits (Supplement)
- An object in an elliptical orbit travels faster when closer to the Sun (perihelion).
- Explanation: conservation of energy.
  - When closer to Sun: gravitational potential energy decreases → kinetic energy increases → speed increases.
  - When farther from Sun: gravitational potential energy increases → kinetic energy decreases → speed decreases.

---

## 6.2 Stars and the Universe

### 6.2.1 The Sun as a Star

#### The Sun (Core)
- The Sun is a star of medium size.
- Composition: mostly hydrogen and helium.
- It radiates most of its energy in:
  - Infrared.
  - Visible light.
  - Ultraviolet.

#### Energy Source of Stars (Supplement)
- Stars are powered by nuclear reactions that release energy.
- In stable stars like the Sun, the main reaction is nuclear fusion.
- Hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium.
- Releases enormous amounts of energy.

---

### 6.2.2 Stars

#### Galaxies (Core)
- A galaxy is a huge collection of billions of stars.
- The Sun is one star in the Milky Way galaxy.
- Other stars in the Milky Way are much further away from Earth than the Sun is.

#### Light-Year (Core)
- A unit of astronomical distance.
- One light-year = distance travelled by light in vacuum in one year.
- Used because distances in space are enormous.
- 1 light-year ≈ \( 9.5 \times 10^{15} \) m (about 9.5 trillion km).

#### Life Cycle of a Star (Supplement)

##### Stage 1: Formation
- Stars form from interstellar clouds of gas and dust containing hydrogen.
- Gravity pulls material together → cloud collapses.
- Temperature increases → forms a protostar.

##### Stage 2: Stable Star
- When the inward force of gravity is balanced by outward pressure from high temperature in the core → stable star (hydrostatic equilibrium).
- Nuclear fusion of hydrogen to helium begins.
- This stage lasts most of the star's life.

##### Stage 3: Red Giant / Red Supergiant
- Eventually, hydrogen in the core runs out.
- The star expands:
  - Most stars (like our Sun) become red giants.
  - More massive stars become red supergiants.

##### Stage 4: Final Stages

**For less massive stars (like the Sun)**
- Red giant → outer layers drift away forming planetary nebula.
- Left behind: white dwarf at centre.
- White dwarf cools over billions of years → black dwarf.

**For massive stars**
- Red supergiant explodes as a supernova.
- Explosion leaves behind neutron star or black hole.
- Nebula from supernova contains new heavier elements.

##### Recycling
- The nebula from a supernova may collapse to form new stars with orbiting planets.
- This is how heavy elements (like iron, gold) are distributed in the universe.

#### Summary Table: Life Cycle of Stars

| Stage                     | Description                                                                 |
|---------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Interstellar cloud        | Cloud of gas (hydrogen) and dust.                                           |
| Protostar                 | Collapsing cloud, temperature rising.                                       |

### 6.2.3 The Universe

#### Structure of the Universe (Core)
- The Milky Way is one of many billions of galaxies.
- All these galaxies together make up the Universe.
- Diameter of the Milky Way: approximately 100,000 light‑years.

#### Redshift (Core)
- Redshift = an increase in the observed wavelength of electromagnetic radiation from a source that is moving away.
- Light from distant galaxies appears redshifted compared to light from Earth.
- Wavelengths are stretched (shifted toward red end of spectrum).

#### Evidence for Expanding Universe (Core)
- Redshift of light from distant galaxies shows they are moving away from us.
- The farther away a galaxy, the greater the redshift (faster it is receding).
- This is evidence that the Universe is expanding.
- Supports the Big Bang Theory – that the Universe began from a single point and has been expanding ever since.

#### Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) (Supplement)
- Microwave radiation of a specific frequency observed coming from all directions in space.
- Origin: produced shortly after the Big Bang (when Universe became transparent).
- As the Universe expanded, the radiation was stretched to longer wavelengths (microwaves).
- CMBR is strong evidence for the Big Bang.

#### Measuring Recession Speed (Supplement)
- The speed \( v \) of a galaxy moving away can be found from its redshift.
- Change in wavelength is proportional to speed.

#### Measuring Distance to Far Galaxies (Supplement)
- The distance \( d \) to a far galaxy can be determined using the brightness of a Type Ia supernova in that galaxy.
- Type Ia supernovae have a known peak luminosity → comparing apparent brightness gives distance.

#### Hubble Constant (Supplement)
- Definition: \( H_0 = \frac{v}{d} \)
  - \( v \) = recession speed of galaxy.
  - \( d \) = distance to galaxy.
- Units: s⁻¹ (or km/s per Mpc, but in exam use per second).
- Current estimate: \( H_0 \approx 2.2 \times 10^{-18} \) s⁻¹.

#### Age of the Universe (Supplement)
- From \( v = H_0 \times d \), we get \( \frac{d}{v} = \frac{1}{H_0} \).
- \( \frac{d}{v} \) is the time since the galaxy started moving (assuming constant speed).
- This gives an estimate of the Universe's age.
- Age ≈ \( 1 / H_0 \approx 14 \) billion years.
- Supports the idea that all matter was once at a single point.

#### Dark Matter and Dark Energy (Extension – not in Core, but useful)
- Dark matter: invisible mass that does not emit light; detected through gravitational effects.
- Dark energy: unknown force causing the expansion of the Universe to accelerate.
- Together, they make up about 95% of the Universe's total mass‑energy.
