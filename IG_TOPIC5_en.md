## 5.1 The Nuclear Model of the Atom

### 5.1.1 Structure of the Atom

- Atoms consist of a **nucleus** (positive) and orbiting **electrons** (negative).
- The nucleus is tiny but contains nearly all the mass.
- **Ions** form by losing electrons (positive ion) or gaining electrons (negative ion).

#### Alpha Particle Scattering Experiment (Gold Foil Experiment)
- **Observations**:
  - Most alpha particles passed straight through.
  - A few were deflected through small angles.
  - About 1 in 8000 was deflected back.
- **Conclusions**:
  - The atom is mostly empty space.
  - Positive charge and mass are concentrated in a very small nucleus.
  - The nucleus is positively charged.

### 5.1.2 Composition of the Nucleus

| Particle | Charge | Relative mass | Location |
|----------|--------|---------------|----------|
| Proton   | +1     | 1             | nucleus  |
| Neutron  | 0      | 1             | nucleus  |
| Electron | –1     | 1/1836        | orbitals |

- **Proton number (Z)** = number of protons → defines the element.
- **Nucleon number (A)** = protons + neutrons. Neutron number = A – Z.
- **Nuclide notation**: written as `^A_Z X`, e.g. `^12_6 C` (carbon-12), `^235_92 U` (uranium-235).
- **Isotopes**: same Z, different A (same number of protons, different neutrons). Same chemical properties, different physical properties (e.g., radioactivity).
- **Nuclear fission**: a heavy nucleus splits into lighter nuclei, releasing energy and neutrons. Example:  
  `^235_92 U + ^1_0 n → ^141_56 Ba + ^92_36 Kr + 3 ^1_0 n` + energy
- **Nuclear fusion**: light nuclei join to form a heavier nucleus, releasing huge energy. Example:  
  `^2_1 H + ^3_1 H → ^4_2 He + ^1_0 n` + energy

## 5.2 Radioactivity

### 5.2.1 Detection of Radioactivity

#### Background Radiation
- Always present. Main sources:
  1. Radon gas (from rocks and soil)
  2. Rocks and buildings (e.g., granite)
  3. Food and drink (e.g., potassium-40 in bananas)
  4. Cosmic rays

#### Detecting Radiation
- **GM tube** (Geiger-Müller tube) + counter measures count rate (counts/s or counts/min).
- **Corrected count rate** = measured count rate – background count rate.

### 5.2.2 The Three Types of Nuclear Emission

| Type | Nature | Ionising power | Penetrating power | Deflection in E/B field | Approx. speed |
|------|--------|----------------|-------------------|-------------------------|---------------|
| α | Helium nucleus (^4_2 He) | strongest | weakest (paper) | towards negative plate, small deflection | 0.1c |
| β | fast electron (^0_-1 e) | medium | medium (few mm Al) | towards positive plate, large deflection | 0.9c |
| γ | high‑energy photon | weakest | strongest (needs thick lead) | not deflected | c |

**Direction of deflection in a magnetic field** (using left‑hand rule):
- **Positive charge (α)**: current direction = direction of motion. Use left‑hand rule (fingers = current, field into palm, thumb = force).
- **Negative charge (β)**: current direction opposite to motion → force opposite to that on a positive charge.
- **γ rays** are uncharged → no deflection.

### 5.2.3 Radioactive Decay

- Decay is **spontaneous** and **random** (cannot predict which nucleus will decay next).

#### Alpha Decay
- Emits α particle (^4_2 He). A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2.
- General equation: `^A_Z X → ^(A-4)_(Z-2) Y + ^4_2 He`
- Example: `^238_92 U → ^234_90 Th + ^4_2 He`

#### Beta Decay (β⁻)
- A neutron in the nucleus turns into a proton and emits an electron: n → p + e⁻
- A unchanged, Z increases by 1.
- General equation: `^A_Z X → ^A_(Z+1) Y + ^0_-1 e`
- Example: `^14_6 C → ^14_7 N + ^0_-1 e`

#### Gamma Emission
- Often accompanies α or β decay. The nucleus drops from a higher to a lower energy state and emits a photon.
- No change in A or Z.

### 5.2.4 Half‑life

- **Definition**: the time taken for half the nuclei in a sample to decay.
- Half‑life is constant for a given isotope (unaffected by temperature, pressure, etc.).
- After n half‑lives, the fraction remaining is `(1/2)^n`. The count rate also decreases by the same factor.
- **Finding half‑life from a decay curve**: choose a point, read the count rate, find the time when the count rate becomes half of that value. The time difference is the half‑life. If background is present, subtract it first.

#### Applications and Choice of Half‑life

| Application | Radiation type | Half‑life requirement | Example |
|-------------|----------------|------------------------|---------|
| Smoke alarm | α | long (hundreds of years) | americium-241 (432 y) |
| Food irradiation | γ | medium (a few years) | cobalt-60 (5.27 y) |
| Sterilising medical equipment | γ | medium | cobalt-60 |
| Thickness control | β | long (stable) | strontium-90 (28.8 y) |
| Cancer treatment (external) | γ | moderate | cobalt-60 |
| Medical tracer (diagnosis) | γ | short (hours) | technetium-99m (6 h) |

### 5.2.5 Safety Precautions

#### Hazards
- Ionising radiation damages DNA → cell death, mutations, cancer. Rapidly dividing cells (e.g., fetus) are most sensitive.

#### Safe Handling and Storage
- Store in shielded containers (e.g., lead).
- Use remote handling tools (tongs, manipulators).
- Dispose of radioactive waste by deep geological burial.
- Wear a **dosimeter** to monitor accumulated dose.

#### Three Principles of Radiation Protection
1. **Time**: minimise exposure time.
2. **Distance**: increase distance (intensity ∝ 1/distance²).
3. **Shielding**:
   - α: paper or gloves – but prevent inhalation/ingestion.
   - β: perspex or a few mm of aluminium (avoid dense materials that cause bremsstrahlung).
   - γ: thick lead or concrete.

## Common Confusions & Memory Aids

- Nucleus is tiny but contains most mass.
- α = helium nucleus, β = electron, γ = photon.
- α decay: A –4, Z –2 ; β decay: A same, Z +1.
- Half‑life is constant; remaining fraction = (1/2)^n.
- Always subtract background from radioactivity measurements.
- Isotopes have same chemical properties but may differ in radioactivity.
