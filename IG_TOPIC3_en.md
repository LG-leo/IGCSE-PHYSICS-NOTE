## Part 1: General Properties of Waves

### 1. What is a wave?

A **wave** is a disturbance that transfers **energy** from one place to another without transferring matter.

- **Example**: Drop a stone into a pond. Ripples spread outward, but a leaf on the water only bobs up and down – it does not move outward with the ripples. Energy travels; the water itself stays.

**Two main types of waves:**

- **Mechanical waves** – need a medium (solid, liquid or gas) to travel. Examples: sound waves, water waves, seismic waves.  
  **Why can't they travel in a vacuum?** A vacuum has no atoms or molecules. Mechanical waves rely on particles bumping into their neighbours to pass on the vibration. Without any particles, there is nothing to vibrate and no way to transfer energy.
- **Electromagnetic waves** – do not need a medium; can travel through a vacuum. Examples: light, radio waves, X-rays.  
  They consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that generate each other. Sunlight reaches Earth across empty space – clear evidence.

### 2. Transverse vs Longitudinal waves (direction of vibration)

#### Transverse wave
- Vibration is **perpendicular** to the direction of energy transfer.
- Visualise: shake one end of a horizontal rope up and down. A bump travels horizontally, while each point on the rope moves only vertically.
- Examples: electromagnetic waves, water ripples, S-waves (earthquake).

#### Longitudinal wave
- Vibration is **parallel** to the direction of energy transfer.
- Visualise: push and pull one end of a horizontal spring. You see regions where coils are close together (compressions) and regions where they are far apart (rarefactions). These alternate regions travel along the spring. Each coil moves back and forth along the same line as the wave.
- Examples: sound waves, P-waves (earthquake).

#### How to identify from a diagram?
- If you see a sine‑wave graph (peaks and troughs) – that is a **transverse** wave representation.
- Longitudinal waves are often drawn as rows of dots: dense dots = compression, sparse dots = rarefaction.

### 3. Describing waves (common terms)

| Term | Definition | Symbol (unit) |
|------|------------|----------------|
| Amplitude | Maximum displacement from equilibrium position. Larger amplitude means more energy. | A (metre, m) |
| Wavelength | Distance between two consecutive points that are in phase (e.g. crest to crest, compression centre to next compression centre). | λ (metre, m) |
| Frequency | Number of complete waves passing a fixed point per second. Unit hertz (Hz), 1 Hz = 1 wave per second. | f (Hz) |
| Period | Time taken for one complete wave to pass a point. | T = 1/f (second, s) |
| Wave speed | Distance the wave travels per second. Speed depends on the medium. | v (m/s) |

**Key formula (for all waves):**
$$ v = f \times \lambda $$
- Example: Speed of sound in air ~340 m/s, frequency = 1000 Hz → wavelength $ \lambda = 340 / 1000 = 0.34 $ m.
- **Unit trap**: if frequency is given in kHz, multiply by 1000 to get Hz; if wavelength is in cm, divide by 100 to get metres.

### 4. Wave behaviours

#### Reflection
Wave bounces off a barrier. **Law of reflection**: angle of incidence = angle of reflection (measured from the normal).

- **Specular reflection**: smooth surface (mirror, calm water). Parallel incident rays stay parallel → clear image.
- **Diffuse reflection**: rough surface (paper, wall). Parallel rays scatter in all directions → object visible from any angle.

#### Refraction
Wave changes direction when it enters a different medium because its speed changes (unless it hits the boundary at 90°).  
**Refraction rule**:
- Speed decreases → wavelength shortens → wave bends **towards** the normal.
- Speed increases → wavelength lengthens → wave bends **away** from the normal.

**Water wave experiment (ripple tank)**: shallow water slows waves. When waves go from deep to shallow, they bend towards the normal.

**Everyday refraction (correct cause)**:
- A pencil in water looks “broken”: light from the pencil travels from **water into air**, speeds up and bends away from the normal. Your eye sees a virtual image shifted from the real position.
- A swimming pool looks shallower than it is: light from the bottom travels from water into air, bends away from the normal, so the virtual image of the bottom is higher.

#### Diffraction
Waves spread out after passing through a narrow gap or around an obstacle.

**Two factors controlling diffraction:**
1. **Gap width (or obstacle size)** – narrower gap gives more diffraction.
2. **Wavelength** – longer wavelength gives more diffraction.

**Exam focus**: Most obvious diffraction occurs when **gap width ≈ wavelength**. Waves then spread into a nearly circular pattern.  
> Why can sound bend around a door but light cannot? Sound wavelength (~1 m) is similar to door gap width (~0.5 m) → noticeable diffraction. Visible light wavelength ~500 nm = 5×10⁻⁷ m, far smaller than the gap → diffraction negligible → light travels in straight lines.

#### Interference (Supplement – qualitative only)
When two waves of the same frequency and constant phase difference overlap, they produce a stable pattern of alternating **constructive** (amplitudes add) and **destructive** (amplitudes cancel) points.  
Interference is unique to waves and shows that wave energy is not just a stream of particles.

## Part 2: Light

### 1. Rectilinear propagation
Light travels in straight lines in a uniform medium. Evidence: pinhole camera, shadows, eclipses.

### 2. Reflection

#### Plane mirror image characteristics (must memorise)
- Virtual (cannot be projected on a screen, appears behind mirror).
- Same size as object.
- Same distance from mirror as object.
- Laterally inverted (left/right swapped).
- Upright.

### 3. Refraction of light (corrected explanations)

**Cause**: speed change when entering a different medium.  
- From air into water/glass: speed decreases, bends **towards** normal.
- From water/glass into air: speed increases, bends **away** from normal.

**Everyday refraction (correct)**
- Pencil “broken” – light from pencil travels from water into air, bends away from normal.
- Pool looks shallower – light from bottom bends away from normal, raising the virtual image.
- Sunrise appears earlier – atmospheric refraction bends sunlight before the Sun is physically above horizon.

### 4. Total Internal Reflection (Supplement – calculation often appears)

#### Conditions (both must be met):
1. Light travels from **denser medium** (higher refractive index, e.g. glass, water) to **less dense medium** (lower refractive index, e.g. air).
2. Angle of incidence **greater than critical angle C**.

#### Critical angle formula
When light goes from medium with refractive index n₁ to medium with n₂ (n₁ > n₂):
$$ \sin C = \frac{n_2}{n_1} $$
If the less dense medium is air (n₂ ≈ 1):
$$ \sin C = \frac{1}{n_1} $$

#### Applications
- **Optical fibres**: light repeatedly totally internally reflects inside the core (higher n) surrounded by cladding (lower n). Signals travel long distances with little loss.
- **Diamond sparkle**: cut so that incoming light undergoes many total internal reflections before escaping from the top.
- **Prism periscopes**: 45° right‑angled prisms reflect light by TIR, much higher efficiency than mirrors.

### 5. Convex lens (Supplement)

#### Basic terms
- Convex lens: thicker in middle, converges light.
- Concave lens: thinner in middle, diverges light.

**Three special rays (for ray diagrams)**:
1. Ray parallel to principal axis → after refraction passes through focal point (convex) or appears to come from focal point (concave).
2. Ray through focal point (or directed toward focal point for concave) → emerges parallel to principal axis.
3. Ray through optical centre → continues straight without bending.

#### Convex lens image formation (must memorise)

| Object distance (u) | Image | Image distance (v) | Application |
|---------------------|-------|--------------------|-------------|
| u > 2f | real, inverted, diminished | f < v < 2f | camera |
| u = 2f | real, inverted, same size | v = 2f | measuring focal length |
| f < u < 2f | real, inverted, magnified | v > 2f | projector |
| u = f | no image (rays emerge parallel) | — | — |
| u < f | virtual, upright, magnified | \|v\| > u (same side as object) | magnifying glass |

> Real image can be projected on a screen; virtual image cannot.

#### Lens formula and magnification (calculations)
$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{u} + \frac{1}{v} $$
$$ m = \frac{\text{image height}}{\text{object height}} = \frac{|v|}{|u|} $$
Sign rules (usually absolute values enough for IGCSE):
- f positive for convex lens, negative for concave.
- v positive for real image, negative for virtual image.

## Part 3: Electromagnetic (EM) Spectrum

### 1. Common properties of EM waves
- All are transverse waves.
- All travel at the same speed in a vacuum: $$ c = 3.0 \times 10^8 \, m/s $$.
- In vacuum: $$ c = f \lambda $$ (higher frequency → shorter wavelength).
- No medium needed – can travel through vacuum.

### 2. Order of EM spectrum (increasing frequency / decreasing wavelength)

**Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X‑ray → Gamma ray**  
Mnemonic: **R M I V U X G**

### 3. Typical applications and hazards (common exam questions)

| Band | Approx. wavelength range | Typical use | Hazard (if overexposed) |
|------|--------------------------|-------------|-------------------------|
| Radio | >0.1 m | broadcasting, TV, mobile | negligible |
| Microwave | 1 mm – 0.1 m | satellite, microwave oven | heating of body tissue |
| Infrared | 700 nm – 1 mm | remote controls, thermal imaging, fibre optics | skin burns |
| Visible | 400 – 700 nm | vision, lighting | eye damage (intense light) |
| Ultraviolet | 10 – 400 nm | security marking, sterilisation, fluorescent lamps | skin cancer, cataracts |
| X‑ray | 0.01 – 10 nm | medical imaging, airport security | cell damage, cancer |
| Gamma | <0.01 nm | cancer radiotherapy, sterilisation of equipment | severe cell damage, cancer |

### 4. Analogue vs digital signals (Supplement)
- **Analogue**: continuously varying signal, prone to noise (e.g. old radio static).
- **Digital**: only two states (0 and 1), noise‑resistant, can be copied perfectly. Used in fibre optics (light on = 1, off = 0) and computers.

## Part 4: Sound

### 1. Production and propagation (connection to vibration)
- **Sound is produced by a vibrating object** (vocal cords, guitar string, loudspeaker cone).  
- The vibration transfers energy to the surrounding medium, creating alternating compressions and rarefactions – a **longitudinal wave**.
- **Needs a medium** – cannot travel through vacuum. Reason: in a vacuum there are no particles to vibrate. Astronauts on the Moon must use radio (EM waves) because sound cannot cross the vacuum.

### 2. Speed of sound in different media
- Solids fastest (strong interparticle forces), e.g. steel ~5000 m/s.
- Liquids slower (water ~1500 m/s, slightly faster in seawater).
- Gases slowest (air ~340 m/s at 20°C). Speed increases with temperature (particles move faster).
- In exams, if not specified, use 330 m/s or 340 m/s for air (as given in the question).

### 3. Hearing and sound qualities
- **Loudness** – depends on amplitude. Larger amplitude → louder sound.
- **Pitch** – depends on frequency. Higher frequency → higher pitch. Human hearing range: 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz (20 kHz).
- **Timbre** – waveform shape, distinguishes different instruments or voices.

### 4. Ultrasound
- Definition: frequency **above 20 kHz**, inaudible to humans.
- Properties: good directionality, reflects off boundaries between different media.
- **Applications**:
  - Medical B‑scan: sends ultrasound pulses into body; echoes from different tissues build an image. Safer than X‑rays (no ionising radiation).
  - Sonar (Sound Navigation and Ranging): measure depth of sea or locate fish. Send pulse, measure return time.
  - Industrial flaw detection: find cracks inside metal without cutting.
  - Ultrasound cleaning: high‑frequency vibrations cause tiny bubbles to collapse, producing shock waves that clean surfaces.

### 5. Echo and distance calculation (common Paper 4 question)
When sound reflects from a distant obstacle (cliff, sea bed), the echo is heard after the total travel time for the round trip.

**Formula**:
$$ d = \frac{v \times t}{2} $$
- d = one‑way distance (m)
- v = speed of sound (m/s)
- t = time between sending pulse and receiving echo (s)

**Example**: A boat sends an ultrasound pulse to the seabed. Echo returns after 0.8 s. Speed of sound in sea water = 1500 m/s. Find depth.  
Depth = (1500 × 0.8) / 2 = 600 m.  
⚠️ Common mistake: forgetting to divide by 2 gives 1200 m (the total path length, not depth).

### 6. Infrasound
- Definition: frequency **below 20 Hz**, inaudible.
- Sources: earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, storms, elephants.
- Properties: very long wavelength, can bend around large obstacles and travel huge distances.

### 7. Doppler effect (Supplement – quantitative understanding)
When the source of waves and the observer move relative to each other, the observed frequency changes.

**Direction‑effect relationship**:
- **Source and observer moving towards each other** → observed frequency **increases** (pitch rises for sound; for light, blue shift).
  - Example: a police car with siren approaching you – you hear a higher pitch.
- **Source and observer moving apart** → observed frequency **decreases** (pitch falls; red shift for light).
  - Example: the same police car moving away – pitch drops.

**Applications**:
- Radar speed traps: emit radio waves, measure frequency shift of the reflected wave to calculate vehicle speed.
- Astronomy: measure red/blue shift of spectral lines to determine whether a star or galaxy is moving away from or towards Earth, and at what speed.

## Summary of Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---------|------------------------|
| Waves transfer matter | No, they transfer energy only. Particles vibrate but stay. |
| Mechanical waves can travel in vacuum | No, they need a medium because vibration must be passed between particles. |
| Transverse vs longitudinal confusion | Check vibration direction relative to propagation: perpendicular → transverse; parallel → longitudinal. |
| Wrong wavelength definition | Distance between two consecutive in‑phase points (e.g. peak to peak, compression to compression). |
| Unit errors in v = fλ | Convert kHz → Hz (×1000), cm → m (÷100) before calculation. |
| All waves need a medium | False – electromagnetic waves do not need a medium. |
| Echo distance forgetting ÷2 | The time measured is for round trip; divide by 2 for one‑way distance. |
| Lens image rules mixed up | Use three special rays to draw image. |
| EM spectrum order | Mnemonic: R M I V U X G. |
| Refraction direction | Air → water: bends **towards** normal (slower medium). Water → air: bends **away** from normal (faster medium). |
| Diffraction conditions wrong | Narrow gap / long wavelength → more diffraction; most obvious when gap ≈ wavelength. |
| Doppler effect direction | Towards → higher frequency; away → lower frequency. |
